A report is your permanent record of what happened during a call or encounter. Writing accurate, professional reports is as important as the patrol work itself — it creates accountability, supports any follow-up actions, and demonstrates the detachment's commitment to realistic policing standards.

## When a report is required

Not every interaction requires a written report, but you must file one for any of the following:

- Any traffic stop resulting in a charge, written warning, or collision
- Any arrest or detainment
- Any call attended where an incident occurred (fight, theft, disturbance, welfare check with findings)
- Any use of force beyond a verbal direction
- Any collision involving a police vehicle
- Any situation directed by a supervisor

<Info>
If you are unsure whether a report is required, err on the side of filing one. It is better to have an unnecessary report on file than to have a significant incident go undocumented.
</Info>

## Report structure

Every report must include the following fields, in this order. Do not omit any field — incomplete reports will be returned for revision.

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Header information" defaultOpen={true}>
    The header identifies who wrote the report, when the incident occurred, and what type of incident it was.

    | Field | Description |
    |---|---|
    | **Date** | The date the incident occurred (not the date the report is written) |
    | **Time** | The time of the incident in 24-hour format (e.g., 14:32) |
    | **Callsign** | Your assigned callsign at the time of the incident |
    | **Incident type** | A brief label: traffic stop, arrest, incident response, use of force, etc. |
    | **Location** | The street address or intersection where the incident occurred |
    | **Involved persons** | Names or descriptions of civilians, suspects, or witnesses |
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Narrative">
    The narrative is the body of your report. It describes what happened in chronological order, from first observation to final disposition.

    Write in **first person** ("I observed…", "I directed the subject to…"). Use past tense throughout. Be factual, specific, and sequential — avoid vague language like "the situation escalated" in favour of specific descriptions like "the subject raised his voice and stepped toward me."

    A complete narrative includes:
    - What prompted the contact or response (the reason for the stop, the nature of the call)
    - What you observed on arrival or approach
    - What was said and done, in order
    - Any evidence collected or charges issued
    - How the situation was resolved
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Outcome">
    The outcome field summarises the result of the incident in one to three sentences. State clearly what action was taken — verbal warning, written notice, arrest, unfounded — and any follow-up required.

    Examples:
    - *"Subject was issued a written warning for speeding. No further action required."*
    - *"Subject was arrested and transported to the detachment. Charges to follow."*
    - *"Call was unfounded upon arrival. No persons located. No further action."*
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Writing style

Your report must be professional, factual, and clear. Avoid the following:

- **Opinions and assumptions** — Report what you observed, not what you think was happening. Write "the subject smelled of alcohol" not "the subject was drunk."
- **Vague language** — Be specific. "The vehicle was travelling at high speed" is weaker than "the vehicle was estimated at 120 km/h in a posted 80 km/h zone."
- **Casual or informal language** — Reports are formal documents. Avoid contractions, slang, or conversational phrasing.
- **Unnecessary detail** — Include everything relevant; exclude everything that is not. Do not pad the narrative with observations that had no bearing on the incident.

<Tip>
Read your report aloud before submitting it. If a sentence sounds awkward or unclear, rewrite it. Your report should be understandable to someone who was not present.
</Tip>

## Common report types

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Traffic stop report">
    Use this format for any traffic stop that results in a charge or written warning.

    **Required fields:** Date, time, callsign, location, vehicle description, plate number, driver information, reason for stop, outcome (charge or warning issued), any relevant observations (e.g., odour of alcohol, no insurance produced).

    **Narrative focus:** Describe why you initiated the stop, what the driver said and produced, and what action you took. If a licence check or plate check was run, include the result.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Arrest report">
    Use this format any time a person is placed under arrest.

    **Required fields:** Date, time, callsign, location, subject information, grounds for arrest, charges, use of force (if applicable), transport destination.

    **Narrative focus:** Describe the events leading to the arrest, the specific grounds used to justify it, what was said to the subject at the time of arrest (including rights and caution if applicable in your community's standards), and any resistance encountered.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Incident response report">
    Use this format for calls attended where an incident occurred — disturbances, collisions, thefts, welfare checks with findings.

    **Required fields:** Date, time, callsign, call type, location, persons involved, findings on arrival, actions taken, outcome.

    **Narrative focus:** Describe what dispatch relayed before arrival, what you found on scene, how you managed the situation, and how it was resolved. Note any witnesses, evidence, or follow-up required.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Submitting reports

Submit all reports using the designated report channel in the detachment Discord server. Use the report submission format pinned in that channel.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Complete the report in full">
    Review every field before submitting. Ensure the narrative is complete and the outcome is clearly stated.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Format the report correctly">
    Use the report template pinned in the report submission channel. Do not submit reports as plain messages — use the template to ensure all fields are present and readable.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Post in the correct channel">
    Submit your report in the designated channel. If your report relates to an ongoing investigation or a senior member's file, notify them directly after submission.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Confirm receipt">
    Check that your report was acknowledged by a supervisor or command staff. If your report has not been acknowledged within 24 hours, follow up in the duty channel.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Warning>
Reports must be submitted within 24 hours of the incident. Late reports require a brief explanation of the delay. Habitually late reports are a conduct issue and will be addressed by a supervisor.
</Warning>

## Tips for accurate and professional reports

- **Write your report immediately after the incident** — Memory degrades quickly. Notes taken on scene will always produce a more accurate report than ones written hours later.
- **Keep notes during patrol** — Jot down plate numbers, names, times, and key observations as they happen. These notes are the source material for your narrative.
- **Be consistent with other reports from the same incident** — If another unit was on scene, coordinate to ensure your reports do not contradict each other on factual matters.
- **Do not fabricate or exaggerate** — A report that is later found to be inaccurate damages your credibility and the detachment's reputation. If you are uncertain about a detail, write what you observed and note the uncertainty.
- **Proofread** — Spelling errors, incomplete sentences, and formatting issues make a report harder to read and undermine its professionalism. Take two minutes to review before submitting.
