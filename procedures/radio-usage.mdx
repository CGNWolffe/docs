The radio is your primary link to dispatch and other units during patrol. Proper radio usage keeps communications clear, reduces confusion during high-pressure situations, and maintains the professional standard expected of every member. Learn these procedures before your first shift and apply them consistently.

## When to use the radio

Use the radio for all official communications during patrol. This includes:

- Going on duty (10-8) and off duty (10-7)
- Calling in a traffic stop
- Notifying dispatch when en route to a call
- Announcing arrival on scene
- Requesting backup or emergency assistance
- Clearing from a call or scene
- Reporting hazards, BOLOs, or significant observations
- Conducting a radio check at the start of shift

<Note>
Use direct (voice) communication with other officers on scene for coordination. The radio is for dispatch communication and updates — not for conversation between units that are physically together.
</Note>

## Proper radio etiquette

Every transmission you make should be clear, concise, and professional. Follow these rules on every call.

**Lead with the recipient, then identify yourself.** Always address dispatch or the unit you are calling before stating your message.
> *"Niagara dispatch, [Callsign]…"*

**Wait for acknowledgement before transmitting a long message.** If you have multiple pieces of information, pause and wait for dispatch to respond with "go ahead" before continuing.

**Speak at a steady pace.** Talking too fast causes words to blur together. Talking too slowly wastes airtime. Find a natural, measured pace.

**Keep transmissions short.** Say what you need to say and stop transmitting. Do not fill silence with unnecessary commentary.

**Never transmit over another unit.** Listen before you key up. If someone else is transmitting, wait until they are finished.

**Use plain language where possible.** Ten-codes are used for common status updates, but plain language is acceptable and often clearer for complex situations. See the [radio codes reference](/communication/radio-codes) for the codes used by this detachment.

**End transmissions with "over" when expecting a reply, and "out" when closing communication.** For routine status updates (going 10-8, going 10-7), "out" is sufficient.

<Warning>
Do not use casual language, humour, or personal conversation on the radio channel. The duty channel is a professional communication channel at all times, regardless of how quiet the shift is.
</Warning>

## Radio check procedure

Perform a radio check at the beginning of every shift before going 10-8. This confirms your audio is working and that dispatch can hear you clearly.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Key up and transmit your check">
    > *"Niagara dispatch, [Callsign], radio check, how do you copy, over?"*
  </Step>
  <Step title="Wait for a response">
    Dispatch or another available unit will confirm signal quality. A typical response:
    > *"[Callsign], Niagara dispatch, loud and clear, over."*
  </Step>
  <Step title="Acknowledge and go 10-8">
    Once you receive a clear confirmation, acknowledge and advise dispatch that you are entering service.
    > *"Copy, [Callsign] is 10-8 from [location]."*
  </Step>
  <Step title="If no response is received">
    Wait 30 seconds and transmit a second check. If still no response, report the issue in the Discord duty channel before continuing. Do not go 10-8 with an unconfirmed radio.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## How to call in a traffic stop

Calling in a traffic stop before approaching the vehicle is mandatory. This creates a record of your location and the vehicle description in case the stop escalates.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Activate your emergency equipment and wait for the vehicle to stop">
    Once the vehicle has come to a complete stop, transmit before you exit your vehicle.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Transmit the stop to dispatch">
    > *"Niagara dispatch, [Callsign], traffic stop, [street address or cross street], [vehicle colour, make, model], [plate number if visible], over."*

    Example:
    > *"Niagara dispatch, 4-Alpha-7, traffic stop, Queen Street and Geneva Street, blue Honda Civic, plate Bravo-Echo-4-7-2, over."*
  </Step>
  <Step title="Wait for dispatch to acknowledge">
    Dispatch will read back the information or advise if the plate returns with anything notable. Do not exit your vehicle until you have received acknowledgement.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Approach the vehicle">
    Once acknowledged, exit your vehicle and approach. Keep your radio accessible in case the situation changes.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Tip>
Spell out plate characters using the phonetic alphabet (Alpha, Bravo, Charlie…) to avoid confusion between similar-sounding letters and numbers. See the radio codes reference for the full phonetic alphabet.
</Tip>

## How to request backup

If a situation exceeds your safe capacity to manage alone, request backup immediately. Do not wait until a situation has deteriorated before calling for support.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Transmit a backup request">
    For non-urgent backup needs:
    > *"Niagara dispatch, [Callsign], requesting backup at [location]. [Brief description], over."*

    Example:
    > *"Niagara dispatch, 4-Alpha-7, requesting backup at 45 Church Street. Subject is non-compliant during a traffic stop, over."*
  </Step>
  <Step title="For immediate officer assistance, transmit a 10-33">
    If you are in danger or require emergency assistance, transmit a 10-33 immediately:
    > *"10-33, 10-33, [Callsign], [location]."*

    All non-emergency radio traffic stops immediately when a 10-33 is broadcast. Every available unit responds.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Maintain your position and update dispatch">
    Unless you must relocate for safety, stay at your last reported position and provide updates to dispatch as the situation develops.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Warning>
A 10-33 is a true emergency signal. Only transmit it when you are in genuine danger or an officer's safety is at risk. Misuse of the emergency signal is a serious conduct violation.
</Warning>

## How to clear from a call

Clearing a call properly closes the loop with dispatch and allows other units and command to track the status of incidents.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Advise dispatch you are clearing">
    > *"Niagara dispatch, [Callsign], clear at [location]. [Disposition]."*

    Example:
    > *"Niagara dispatch, 4-Alpha-7, clear at Queen and Geneva. One issued a written warning. 10-8."*
  </Step>
  <Step title="State your status and next intention">
    If you are resuming patrol, advise that you are 10-8. If you are going off duty, advise 10-7. If you are en route to another call, say so.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Wait for dispatch to acknowledge">
    Do not consider yourself cleared until dispatch acknowledges. If dispatch does not respond within a reasonable interval, re-transmit.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Common mistakes to avoid

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Transmitting before checking if the channel is clear">
    Always listen for a moment before keying up. Transmitting over another unit causes both messages to become unintelligible. If you accidentally transmit over someone, wait for them to finish and then re-transmit your message.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Calling in a traffic stop after approaching the vehicle">
    The stop must be called in before you exit your vehicle. Approaching first and calling in second means dispatch has no record of your location if the stop escalates in the first few seconds.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Using vague location descriptions">
    "On the highway" or "near downtown" are not adequate. Provide a street address, intersection, or kilometre marker. If you are unsure of the exact location, give the closest known landmark and follow up when you can confirm.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Talking too fast or mumbling">
    In a high-stress situation, the urge to rush your transmission is natural — resist it. Dispatch and other units need to understand your message on the first transmission. If you have to repeat yourself, you have already lost time.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Leaving the radio open or transmitting dead air">
    An open microphone blocks the channel. If your push-to-talk activates accidentally, release it immediately and transmit a brief "disregard" to clear the air. Check your keybind settings if this happens frequently.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Using casual or out-of-character language">
    Phrases like "yeah", "sounds good", or "my bad" have no place on the duty channel. If you make an error in a transmission, simply re-transmit the correct version and continue professionally.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
