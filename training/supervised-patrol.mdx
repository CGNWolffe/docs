Supervised patrol is the phase between completing your FTO sign-off and earning clearance for fully independent patrol. You handle calls and radio traffic as the primary officer, but an experienced member accompanies you and can step in if needed. This page explains how long supervised patrol lasts, what your supervisor assesses, and what it takes to move on to independent patrol.

## What supervised patrol is and how long it lasts

After your FTO signs you off, you are cleared to patrol — but not alone. Supervised patrol exists because FTO training, however thorough, is a controlled environment. Supervised patrol exposes you to real, unscripted scenarios with a safety net still in place.

During this phase you operate as the primary officer on all calls you attend. Your supervisor does not handle calls for you. They observe, take notes, and debrief you afterward. Think of it as your FTO evaluation phase, but with full operational responsibility on your shoulders.

**Supervised patrol typically lasts a minimum of five patrol sessions.** There is no maximum — your supervisor advances you when they are satisfied with your consistent performance, not when you have hit a session count. Sessions must be at least one hour of active patrol to count toward the minimum.

<Note>
Supervised patrol sessions must be logged in the CAD system with your supervisor's callsign recorded as the secondary unit. Sessions without a CAD log do not count toward your progression.
</Note>

## Expectations during supervised patrol

You are expected to patrol to the full standard of a Trained Constable from your very first supervised session. Your supervisor is not there to repeat FTO training. They are there to confirm that the skills you demonstrated during sign-off hold up under normal patrol conditions.

Specific expectations during supervised patrol include:

- **In-service and out-of-service callouts** — announce every change of status on the radio without prompting
- **Traffic stop announcements** — call in every stop with plate, location, and unit number before approaching the vehicle
- **Report filing** — complete and submit all reports before going out-of-service from any call
- **Supervisor communication** — brief your supervisor after each significant call; do not wait for them to debrief you
- **Professionalism** — maintain in-character conduct consistent with OPP standards at all times

<Warning>
Falling below FTO sign-off standard during supervised patrol — for example, repeatedly missing radio callouts or filing incomplete reports — may result in your supervised patrol period being extended and a note on your record.
</Warning>

## How to request a supervisor for a patrol session

You are responsible for arranging your own supervised patrol sessions. You cannot simply log on and patrol; you must confirm that a qualified supervisor is available before going in-service.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Check supervisor availability">
    Look in **#supervisor-availability** on Discord. Supervisors post their available windows there. You can also ping the **@Supervisor** role to ask for current availability, but do not do this during an active patrol when supervisors may be engaged.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Confirm with your supervisor">
    Send a direct message to the available supervisor to confirm they are willing to cover your session. Include your planned start time and how long you expect to patrol.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Coordinate in the patrol channel">
    Before going in-service, post in **#patrol-log** with your unit number, your supervisor's callsign, and your planned patrol area. Tag your supervisor so they can confirm.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Log in CAD">
    When you go in-service, open a shift log in CAD and add your supervisor as the secondary unit. This is the official record of your supervised patrol session.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Debrief after the session">
    At the end of your session, go out-of-service with your supervisor and conduct a verbal debrief. Your supervisor will note the session in their supervisor log and provide written or verbal feedback.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Tip>
Try to schedule sessions at times when there is decent server activity. Supervised patrol on a quiet server with minimal calls gives your supervisor very little to evaluate. Aim for peak hours when possible.
</Tip>

## What supervisors assess

Supervisors use the same four competency areas as the FTO program, but the bar is higher. You are no longer a trainee — you are a constable expected to perform consistently without coaching.

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Radio discipline">
    Supervisors assess whether you use radio correctly without prompting. This means proper 10-codes, concise transmissions, correct identification format, and responding promptly to dispatch. Common issues at this stage include forgetting to announce traffic stops and transmitting unnecessary commentary on the main channel.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Scene management">
    Can you control a scene independently? Supervisors watch for officer safety positioning on traffic stops, situational awareness at active calls, and how you handle escalating situations. They also look at whether you request backup at the right moments — both too late and unnecessarily early are noted.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Report accuracy and timeliness">
    Reports must be accurate, complete, and filed before you go out-of-service. Supervisors check your submitted reports after each call. Fields left blank, incorrect codes, or narrative sections that do not match what occurred are all flagged. Late reports — filed after you have already signed off — count against you.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Initiative and judgment">
    Independent patrol requires you to make decisions without instruction. Supervisors assess whether you are proactive about patrol activities, whether you make sound calls under pressure, and whether you escalate appropriately to command when a situation warrants it.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Professionalism and conduct">
    This covers both in-character and out-of-character behaviour. In character: how you speak to civilians, your tone under stress, and your adherence to OPP conduct standards. Out of character: how you communicate with your supervisor during the session, your attitude toward feedback, and your Discord conduct.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## How to graduate from supervised patrol to independent patrol

Advancement to independent patrol requires a formal recommendation from your supervisor and sign-off from the FTO Coordinator or a command member.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Complete the minimum sessions">
    Ensure you have at least five qualifying supervised patrol sessions logged in CAD. Each session must be at least one hour of active patrol with a supervisor callsign recorded.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Request a progression review">
    After your fifth session — or at any point you feel ready — ask your supervisor to submit a progression review. Do this via direct message; do not post progression requests in public channels.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Supervisor submits their report">
    Your supervisor completes a progression report covering your performance across all five competency areas. They submit this to the FTO Coordinator. You are not required to do anything during this step, but you may be asked follow-up questions.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Coordinator review">
    The FTO Coordinator reviews the report and your CAD logs. If everything is in order, they approve the advancement and update your Discord roles. This typically takes 24–48 hours.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Receive independent patrol clearance">
    You receive the **Constable** role on Discord and are cleared for solo patrol. A notification is posted in **#roster-updates**. You can now go in-service independently without requiring a supervisor present.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Info>
Advancement is not automatic at five sessions. If your supervisor does not yet feel confident in your consistency, they will continue supervised sessions and work with you on specific areas before submitting a progression review.
</Info>

## Common feedback from supervisors and how to improve

These are the issues supervisors most frequently raise during supervised patrol, and what you can do about each one.

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Forgetting to announce traffic stops on the radio">
    This is the single most common issue at this stage. It is usually a habit gap, not a knowledge gap — you know you need to do it, but you forget in the moment. Fix this by building a checklist habit: before you turn on your lights, say the callout out loud. After a few sessions it becomes automatic. If it keeps happening, your supervisor may ask you to announce stops before you activate lights rather than after.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Reports filed late or with missing fields">
    Late reports typically happen when officers go out-of-service before completing their paperwork. Get into the habit of treating report completion as part of the call — the call is not over until the report is submitted. For missing fields, re-read the [report writing SOP](/procedures/report-writing) and keep it open in a second window while you file.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Not requesting backup soon enough">
    New constables often hesitate to call for backup because they do not want to look like they cannot handle a situation. Requesting backup is not a sign of weakness — it is correct officer safety procedure. If a stop produces a second occupant, if a subject is non-compliant, or if a scene is ambiguous, call for backup. Your supervisor will always note late backup requests; they will never penalise a timely one.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Losing control of a scene">
    If a supervisor observes you losing control of a scene — multiple subjects moving at once, failing to maintain perimeter, or becoming reactive rather than directing — they will step in and debrief you on scene management principles afterward. Before your next session, review the patrol procedures SOP and specifically the section on multi-occupant traffic stops and active call response.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Flat or procedural tone with civilians">
    Officers who recite procedures robotically tend to escalate situations that did not need to escalate. Supervisors look for natural but professional communication. You do not need to be overly friendly, but you do need to sound like a real officer rather than a script reader. Reviewing recordings of your own sessions — if you record them — is the fastest way to hear this habit and correct it.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
