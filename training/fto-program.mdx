The Field Training Officer (FTO) program is the structured pathway every new member completes before patrolling independently. Your FTO rides alongside you, evaluates your performance across core competency areas, and signs off once you demonstrate the standard the detachment expects. This page tells you everything you need to know — from your first ride-along to the moment you're cleared for solo patrol.

## What the FTO program is and why it exists

New members join with varying levels of experience. Some have patrolled in other communities; others have never touched a police roleplay server. The FTO program creates a consistent baseline so that every officer on patrol — regardless of background — operates the same way and holds the same standard.

The program protects both you and the community. It gives you a guided, lower-pressure environment to learn detachment-specific procedures before you're responsible for managing incidents alone. It gives the detachment confidence that its members can handle real patrol situations correctly.

Completion of the FTO program is **mandatory** before you are permitted to conduct any independent or supervised solo patrol.

<Note>
The FTO program is not a punishment or a barrier. It is a resource. Your FTO's job is to help you succeed, not to catch you making mistakes.
</Note>

## How to get matched with an FTO

Once you have completed the new member onboarding steps (Discord role, application approval, and detachment orientation), you are eligible to be assigned an FTO.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Post in #fto-requests">
    Head to the **#fto-requests** channel on the OPP Discord server and post a brief availability request. Include your timezone and the days and times you are generally free.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Wait for assignment">
    The FTO Coordinator reviews incoming requests and matches you with an available FTO based on schedule compatibility. This typically takes 24–72 hours. You will be pinged directly when a match is made.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Confirm with your FTO">
    Once matched, send your assigned FTO a direct message to confirm your first session time. Be prompt — failing to respond within 48 hours may result in reassignment.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Attend your first session">
    Join the patrol at the agreed time. Your FTO will brief you before going in-service. Have your CAD access ready and your radio set up before the session starts.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Info>
If scheduling conflicts arise after you have been matched, message your FTO directly and notify the FTO Coordinator. Do not simply miss a session without communication.
</Info>

## What the FTO evaluation covers

Your FTO assesses you across four core competency areas throughout your ride-alongs. There is no single pass/fail moment — your FTO builds a picture of your performance over multiple sessions.

### Radio usage

You are expected to use correct 10-codes, speak clearly and concisely, identify your unit and division before transmitting, and yield the radio appropriately. Excessive chatter, missing required callouts (such as in-service, out-of-service, and traffic stop announcements), and failing to respond to dispatch are all flagged.

### Patrol procedures

This covers how you conduct traffic stops, respond to calls, approach scenes, and manage suspects. Your FTO looks at situational awareness, decision-making, and whether you follow detachment SOPs. You should have read the [patrol procedures](/procedures/patrol) documentation before your first session.

### Report writing

After incidents you will be required to complete reports in CAD. Your FTO reviews your reports for accuracy, completeness, correct formatting, and timely submission. Reports must be filed before going out-of-service from any call.

### Professionalism

Professionalism covers your in-character conduct, how you treat civilians and other officers, your adherence to the chain of command, punctuality to sessions, and your behaviour in OOC communication during patrol. This includes how you respond to feedback from your FTO.

<Tip>
Ask your FTO for feedback after each session — not just at the end of the program. Small corrections early are much easier to address than patterns that have solidified over multiple ride-alongs.
</Tip>

## Stages of the FTO program

The program runs in three stages. Your FTO determines when you are ready to progress from one stage to the next — there is no fixed minimum number of hours, though most trainees complete the full program across three to five sessions.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Ride-along phase">
    In your first session you ride as a passenger and observer. You do not take primary on any call. Your FTO handles all radio traffic, reports, and scene management. Your job is to watch, ask questions, and absorb how the detachment operates. This phase typically covers one full patrol session.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Evaluation phase">
    You take the lead on calls and traffic stops while your FTO observes. Your FTO may step in if a situation is escalating unsafely or if a critical error needs immediate correction, but otherwise lets you work independently. After each call, your FTO gives you a debrief. This phase continues until your FTO is confident you meet the standard across all four competency areas.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Sign-off">
    When your FTO determines you are ready, they submit a sign-off report to the FTO Coordinator. You receive the Trained Constable role on Discord and are cleared to begin supervised patrol. Your FTO will notify you directly that you have passed.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## What happens if you don't pass

If your FTO determines you have not yet met the standard, they will not sign you off at the end of the evaluation phase. This is not a failure — it is feedback.

Your FTO will document specific areas where you need improvement and schedule additional sessions. The program has no hard limit on how many sessions you can take. Most trainees who are extended simply need more repetitions on specific scenarios.

<Warning>
If you are extended, you must address the documented areas before requesting re-evaluation. Attending additional sessions without working on the flagged items will not result in a sign-off.
</Warning>

In rare cases where a trainee demonstrates behaviour that is incompatible with detachment standards — such as repeated insubordination, dishonesty, or conduct unbecoming of an officer — the FTO Coordinator may refer the case to command for a review. This is uncommon and reserved for serious conduct issues, not skill deficiencies.

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="What if I disagree with my FTO's assessment?">
    If you believe your evaluation was inaccurate or unfair, you may submit a written appeal to the FTO Coordinator via direct message on Discord. Describe the specific concern clearly and professionally. Appeals are reviewed by the Coordinator and, if warranted, a second FTO may observe an additional session. Appeals based solely on disagreeing with the outcome — without citing a specific procedural error — are unlikely to succeed.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Can I request a different FTO?">
    You may request a reassignment if you have a documented scheduling incompatibility or a genuine conflict of interest. Requests based on not getting along with your FTO are handled on a case-by-case basis. Contact the FTO Coordinator to discuss.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="How long does the program take?">
    Most trainees complete the program in three to five sessions of one to two hours each. There is no expiry deadline, but extended inactivity (more than two weeks without a scheduled session) may result in your application being placed on hold.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Do I need to complete ride-alongs in one go?">
    No. Sessions can be scheduled across multiple days or weeks. However, try to maintain momentum — trainees who space sessions too far apart often have to re-cover earlier material.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## How to become an FTO

If you want to help train incoming members, you can apply to become an FTO once you meet the requirements below.

### Requirements

- Minimum 30 days of active service since FTO sign-off
- No active disciplinary actions or command notes
- Demonstrated proficiency across all four evaluation competency areas during your own patrol record
- Recommendation from a current FTO or supervisor (or self-nomination with a written statement)

### Application process

<Steps>
  <Step title="Review the FTO handbook">
    Read the FTO Handbook pinned in **#fto-staff** before applying. The handbook covers evaluation standards, report templates, and how to give effective debrief feedback.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Submit your application">
    Post your application in **#fto-applications** using the provided template. Include your service record summary, why you want to be an FTO, and your availability.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Shadow an active FTO">
    Once your application is acknowledged, you shadow an experienced FTO for a minimum of two full sessions. You observe and take notes — you do not evaluate the trainee during this phase.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Complete the FTO assessment">
    The FTO Coordinator runs a short written assessment covering evaluation criteria, documentation requirements, and escalation procedures. Pass rate is 80%.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Receive your FTO designation">
    On passing, you receive the **Field Training Officer** role on Discord and are added to the FTO roster. You are then eligible to be matched with incoming trainees.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Info>
FTO status is a voluntary role that comes with real responsibility. FTOs are evaluated periodically based on the performance of their trainees and the quality of their sign-off reports. Consistently poor trainee outcomes result in a review conversation with the Coordinator.
</Info>

## Tips for success during FTO training

<Columns cols={2}>
  <Card title="Read the SOPs first" icon="book-open">
    Before your first session, read the [patrol procedures](/procedures/patrol) and [radio usage](/procedures/radio-usage) pages in full. Your FTO expects you to arrive with foundational knowledge — they are not there to teach you to read.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Communicate your gaps" icon="message-circle">
    If there is something you genuinely do not know, tell your FTO. Attempting to bluff through a scenario you don't understand wastes both your time and creates bad habits.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Take notes between sessions" icon="pencil">
    After each session, write down the corrections your FTO gave you. Review them before the next session. This is the single most effective habit you can build during training.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Treat it as real" icon="shield">
    The more seriously you treat FTO sessions as real patrol situations, the more you get out of them. Officers who treat ride-alongs casually tend to make the same casual errors once they're on their own.
  </Card>
</Columns>
