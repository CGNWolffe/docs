Before you patrol on your own, you need to complete six steps in order. Each one builds on the last — skipping ahead will leave you without the knowledge or authorisation you need to operate correctly. Work through this checklist from top to bottom and confirm each step is done before moving to the next.

<Note>
  If you get stuck on any step, ask a supervisor or senior member in the appropriate Discord channel. Do not proceed past a step you have not completed.
</Note>

<Steps>
  <Step title="Join the OPP Discord">
    The Discord server is the central hub for all detachment activity. Announcements, shift coordination, training scheduling, and member communication all happen there. Without it, you cannot receive your callsign or access the resources you need.

    Use the invite link provided by whoever recruited you, or find the current link pinned in a public detachment channel. Once you join:

    - Read the rules channel in full before posting anywhere.
    - Accept the server rules when prompted — this grants you access to member channels.
    - Introduce yourself in the designated introductions channel if one exists.

    <Warning>
      Do not share the Discord invite link publicly without authorisation from a supervisor. Membership is managed to maintain detachment quality.
    </Warning>
  </Step>

  <Step title="Receive your callsign">
    Your callsign is your official identifier within the detachment. You use it every time you transmit on radio, sign a report, or identify yourself in any official capacity. It ties your activity to your member record and allows supervisors to track patrol assignments.

    After joining Discord, post in the callsign request channel (or equivalent) and a supervisor will assign you one. Callsigns follow a structured format — do not create your own or use a callsign that has not been officially assigned to you.

    Once assigned:

    - Update your Discord nickname to include your callsign as directed.
    - Record it somewhere accessible — you will use it constantly.
    - Review the [callsigns reference](/communication/callsigns) to understand how the format works.

    <Info>
      Callsign assignment may take up to 24 hours depending on supervisor availability. Be patient and check back if you have not received one after that window.
    </Info>
  </Step>

  <Step title="Review the SOP">
    The Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) define how every member of the detachment conducts themselves in the field. Reading them before your first patrol is not optional — it is a prerequisite for FTO training. Your FTO trainer will assume you have read and understood the core procedures.

    Start with these pages in this order:

    1. [Procedures overview](/procedures/overview) — understand the structure and scope of the SOPs.
    2. [Patrol SOP](/procedures/patrol) — the primary reference for in-field conduct.
    3. [Radio usage SOP](/procedures/radio-usage) — how to communicate correctly on the radio.
    4. [Report writing SOP](/procedures/report-writing) — how to document patrol incidents.

    Take notes as you read. If something is unclear, ask in Discord before your FTO session rather than during it.

    <Tip>
      Bookmark the patrol and radio usage pages so you can reference them quickly during active sessions. You will consult them often in the early weeks.
    </Tip>
  </Step>

  <Step title="Learn radio usage">
    Radio is your primary communication tool in the field. Correct radio discipline keeps the channel clear, ensures other members can understand you, and reflects the professionalism of the detachment. Incorrect usage — talking over other units, using the wrong codes, or failing to identify yourself — creates confusion and slows down operations.

    Before your FTO session, make sure you can do the following without referring to notes:

    - Identify yourself by callsign at the start of every transmission.
    - Use the correct 10-codes for common situations (see [radio codes reference](/communication/radio-codes)).
    - Keep transmissions short and clear — one piece of information per transmission.
    - Wait for a clear channel before transmitting.
    - Acknowledge transmissions directed at you promptly.

    Practice with a trusted member or supervisor before your first live patrol if possible. Radio fluency takes repetition — the earlier you start, the faster it becomes natural.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Complete FTO training">
    Field Training Officer (FTO) training is a structured programme where an experienced member guides you through your first patrols directly. Your FTO will observe how you apply the SOPs, coach you on radio usage and patrol conduct, and sign off on your readiness to operate with supervision.

    To schedule your FTO session:

    - Post in the FTO scheduling channel on Discord (or contact a supervisor directly).
    - Confirm you have completed steps 1–4 above — your FTO will ask.
    - Show up on time for the scheduled session.

    During the session, your FTO evaluates:

    - Radio conduct and correct use of codes.
    - Patrol procedure adherence.
    - Report writing quality.
    - Situational awareness and decision-making.

    <Warning>
      If you skip or rush steps 1–4 before your FTO session, your trainer may pause the session until you are properly prepared. This delays your progression — prepare thoroughly beforehand.
    </Warning>

    For full details on what FTO training involves and how sign-off works, see the [FTO programme page](/training/fto-program).
  </Step>

  <Step title="Begin supervised patrol">
    After passing FTO training, you move to supervised patrol. You operate in the field independently but with a senior member or supervisor monitoring your activity and available to provide guidance. This stage bridges the gap between training and fully independent operation.

    During supervised patrol:

    - Conduct yourself exactly as you did during FTO — there is no reduced standard.
    - Submit reports for every patrol session as required by the patrol SOP.
    - Ask your supervisor if you encounter a situation not covered by the SOPs.
    - Expect feedback after sessions — act on it.

    Supervised patrol continues until your supervisor determines you are ready to operate independently. The length varies depending on how consistently you apply procedures. There is no fixed timeline.

    <Check>
      Once your supervisor confirms you have completed supervised patrol successfully, you are a fully operational member of OPP GLJRP Niagara Detachment. Welcome to the unit.
    </Check>
  </Step>
</Steps>
